 # Sustainability Model for the Open Educational Library

## Introduction

For an open-source educational library to remain successful, it requires a sustainable funding strategy. Since the software is free and openly available, sustainability must rely on alternative economic models that support maintenance, security updates, community management, and long-term development.

This document evaluates two common Open Source Software (OSS) sustainability approaches:

1. Red Hat Service-Based Model
2. Foundation-Driven Model

---

## 1. Red Hat Service-Based Model

### Description

The Red Hat model is based on offering free open-source software while generating revenue through professional services.

Instead of charging for the software itself, organizations pay for:

- Technical support
- Staff training
- Installation services
- Security maintenance
- Premium documentation
- Enterprise customization

### Advantages

- Generates stable revenue
- Encourages professional support
- Improves reliability and trust
- Allows institutions to receive expert assistance

### Disadvantages

- Requires skilled support staff
- Smaller organizations may not afford premium services
- Heavy dependence on enterprise clients

### Application to this Library

For this educational library, the service-based model could include:

- Teacher training workshops
- Technical support for schools
- Custom educational modules
- Hosting and maintenance services
- Deployment assistance for government institutions

Schools could use the library for free while institutions needing advanced support could pay for professional services.

---

## 2. Foundation-Driven Model

### Description

The foundation-driven model depends on nonprofit support, grants, donations, partnerships, and community contributions.

Funding may come from:

- Government grants
- Educational NGOs
- University partnerships
- International donors
- Community donations

### Advantages

- Keeps software completely open and accessible
- Encourages community participation
- Reduces financial barriers for schools
- Suitable for public-sector education projects

### Disadvantages

- Funding can be unpredictable
- Dependence on donors
- Limited long-term financial certainty

### Application to this Library

This library could partner with:

- Universities
- Ministries of Education
- NGOs supporting digital learning
- Community developers

Funding would support:

- Feature development
- Bug fixes
- Documentation
- Teacher onboarding
- Student accessibility improvements

---

## Comparison of the Two Models

| Factor | Red Hat Model | Foundation Model |
|--------|---------------|------------------|
| Revenue Stability | High | Medium |
| Accessibility | Medium | High |
| Community Participation | Medium | High |
| Government Suitability | High | Very High |
| Long-Term Risk | Lower | Higher |

---

## Recommended Approach

A hybrid approach is recommended.

The library should remain fully open source while using:

- Foundation grants for educational access
- Service contracts for advanced institutional support

This approach balances sustainability, accessibility, and long-term growth.

## Conclusion

The sustainability of this educational library depends on balancing openness with financial support. Combining service-based revenue and foundation funding creates a practical strategy for long-term maintenance and educational impact.
